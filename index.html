Who it serves

Three sides of every real requirements effort. One platform.

Requirements rarely fail inside the document. They fail in the gaps between the team writing it, the experts reviewing it, and the partners relaying feedback. ReqPub gives each group its own surface and keeps every exchange on the record.

The build team

Write together, in the same document, at the same time

Everyone sees everyone's work live. Presence shows who is in which field, every edit is saved under its author's name, and simultaneous edits cannot overwrite each other. When two people collide on the same field, the platform says so and keeps both versions in view.

How it works

From a blank page to an approved baseline in four moves

1

Answer the worksheet together

A guided intake covers users, scope, requirements, data, and AI evaluation. The document builds itself beside you while the whole team edits live.

2

Generate a version

One click locks the worksheet into an immutable, numbered baseline with the author's name and an automatic change summary, down to which requirement IDs moved.

3

Collect the outside view

Review briefs for SMEs, testing links for pilot users, a portal for partners. Every response lands in one inbox, against the exact version it reviewed.

4

Approve and hand off

Named approvers sign off in the app. A version cannot be marked Approved while anyone is pending. Export with approvals and history on the cover.

What it does

Everything a requirement needs before engineering builds from it

The platform enforces the structure. Your team supplies the judgment.

True multiplayer editing

Live presence shows who is typing where, and every save is checked by the server. When two people add requirements in the same second, both land with distinct permanent IDs.

Approvals that actually gate

Versions move from Draft to In review to Approved, with named approvers on each. The rule is enforced: nothing carries the Approved status while a sign-off is still pending.

Testable by construction

Every requirement carries a permanent ID and a measurable fit criterion, so the technology team knows exactly what done means. Acceptance stops being a debate.

AI evaluation criteria

Probabilistic features get golden-dataset metrics, hallucination guardrails, and numeric thresholds. A single expected output cannot verify a feature whose output varies.

Feedback that becomes requirements

SME notes, reviews, and pilot reports arrive in one live inbox. Triage each item, reply to the author in a thread, and promote the best directly into the spec with one click.

Change you can defend

Every baseline is compared with the last: added, modified, and removed requirements listed by ID, with the author's name on the version. Answering what changed since the client saw it takes one click.

Why teams trust it

Built to pass the questions your client's engineers ask first.

The collaboration layer uses the same architecture pattern as Figma and Linear: the server orders every change and resolves conflicts at the field level. Around it sits the paper trail enterprise reviewers expect.

An audit trail nobody can edit

Every edit, version, status change, and inbound submission is recorded by the database itself, with names and timestamps. The log is append-only and cannot be altered from the app.

Isolation enforced in the database

Row-level security scopes every query to your organization. Partners see only their assigned briefs. SMEs see curated summaries that exclude fit criteria, schedules, and internal notes.

Baselines that cannot drift

Versions are immutable snapshots with server-allocated numbers. Two managers generating at the same moment get v1.4 and v1.5, never two v1.4s.

Every save confirmed, every failure visible

Writes are confirmed by the server and retried on flaky networks, with the state always on screen: saving, saved, or failed with a retry button. Concurrent edits are detected and resolved by name.

Hand-off

Leaves the platform as cleanly as it was written
